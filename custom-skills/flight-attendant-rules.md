# Delta Flight Attendant Rules & FAA Part 121

How Delta flight attendant (FA) schedules are governed. The critical structural
fact: **Delta flight attendants are not unionized**, so unlike the pilots (whose
rules come from the ALPA PWA), FA pay and work rules are **set by the company**
(Delta Inflight Service) on top of the **FAA Part 121** legal floor. Treat the
unionization question neutrally; the rules below are current company policy plus
regulation and can change.

## 1. Governance: company policy + FAA, not a contract

Because Delta FAs have no collective bargaining agreement, their pay rates, work
rules, guarantees, and benefits are determined unilaterally by Delta and can be
changed by the company (within the FAA floor and applicable law). This differs
fundamentally from a CBA workgroup:

- There is no contractual "rig" or grievance/arbitration structure guaranteeing
  scheduling terms — protections come from FAA regulation and company policy.
- An **AFA-CWA organizing campaign** has sought to unionize Delta FAs; as of
  mid-2026 there is no ratified contract. If that changes, the governance model
  changes. Describe this factually and even-handedly without advocacy.

Analytically, this means FA scheduling rules are more changeable than the pilots'
and must be checked against current Delta Inflight work-rule documents rather than
a stable contract.

## 2. FAA Part 121: the legal floor

The binding regulatory constraints on FA duty and staffing:

- **Rest (§121.467).** Following the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2018 and the FAA's
  2022 final rule, a flight attendant scheduled to a duty period of **14 hours or
  less must receive at least 10 consecutive hours of scheduled rest, and that rest
  may not be reduced under any circumstances** (the prior rule was 9 hours,
  reducible to 8). Longer scheduled duty periods carry their own (longer) minimum
  rest provisions under §121.467 — confirm the exact values in the regulation for
  duties over 14 and over 18 hours.
- **Minimum staffing (§121.391).** The number of FAs required on board scales with
  seating capacity (broadly, one per unit of ~50 seats), with type-specific minima.
  Staffing above the minimum is permitted and common on premium/widebody flying.
- **Duty/rest definitions.** Duty and rest are defined by the regulation; rest is
  the interval between duty periods and is distinct from layover usage or
  commuting.

These are floors. A company policy may be more generous, but never less.

## 3. Schedule structure: lineholders and reserve

Like pilots, FAs split into:

- **Lineholders** — hold a monthly **line** of sequences (the FA term for trips/
  pairings: a multi-leg, often multi-day rotation from base), bid by seniority,
  with more schedule control (choice of trips, layovers, days off) and the ability
  to swap/drop.
- **Reserve** — on-call to cover sick calls, cancellations, and operational needs;
  more common for junior FAs. Includes airport/standby ("Ready Reserve") and on-call
  reserve, with availability windows and a reserve guarantee.

Typical monthly shape: roughly 65–90 flight (credit) hours and ~12–14 days off,
varying by seniority, base, and bidding.

## 4. Pay structure and boarding pay

- FAs are paid by the hour on a **credit-hour** basis (historically beginning at
  door close / brake release), with a monthly pay basis built around scheduled
  flight hours plus boarding pay.
- **Boarding pay** — in 2022 Delta became the first major U.S. carrier to pay FAs
  during boarding, at **half the hourly rate** for scheduled boarding time. This is
  a notable non-union policy and a real component of monthly pay (e.g. pay bases
  quoted as ~90 flight hours plus a boarding-pay allotment).
- Additional pay elements: **per diem** (untaxed on overnight trips), **lead/purser
  premiums**, **language-qualified** pay where applicable, holiday/incentive
  premiums, and **profit sharing** (Delta's is historically large). Top-of-scale
  hourly rates are in the low-to-mid $80s/hour range as of 2025 — verify current
  rates, which Delta adjusts.

Because these are company-set, pay and premium specifics move more often than a
contract workgroup's; treat published figures as point-in-time.

## 5. Positions, qualifications, and staffing

- **Lead FA / purser** roles carry extra responsibility and premium pay; widebody
  international flying uses pursers and larger crews.
- **Language of Destination (LOD)** / language-qualified FAs are required or
  preferred on certain international routes and may carry premium pay.
- Crew size on a given flight is the FAA §121.391 minimum or more, depending on
  cabin configuration and service requirements.

## 6. Scheduling mechanics

- **Bidding** by seniority for lines/positions/bases each bid period.
- **Trip trading / open time** — FAs adjust schedules via a trip-trade system and
  an open-time board (pick up, drop, swap), subject to legality and coverage —
  analogous in spirit to the pilots' PCS/swap tools but under company rules.
- **Reserve assignment** — reserves are assigned open flying under company reserve
  rules and FAA legality (the 10-hour rest floor binds here too).
- **Premium/incentive open time** — Delta uses incentive pay to cover hard-to-staff
  flying during irregular operations and peaks.

## 7. What to verify

Confirm against current **Delta Inflight Service work-rule documents** and the
**FARs**: exact monthly guarantees and pay bases, current hourly rates and boarding-
pay allotment, reserve availability rules, premium/lead/LOD pay, sick/vacation
accrual, and the precise §121.467 rest values for longer duty periods. Also verify
the current status of any AFA representation effort. FA rules are company policy
and shift more readily than a CBA — always date your figures.
